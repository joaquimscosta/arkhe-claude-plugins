## Two-Sided Market Dynamics

When analyzing a marketplace idea, consider:

- **Chicken-and-egg problem**: Marketplaces need both supply and demand — which side do you seed first?
- **Supply-side strategy**: Typically easier to start with supply (sellers, providers, creators)
- **Demand aggregation**: How do you attract buyers to a marketplace with limited supply?
- **Liquidity**: The point where the marketplace consistently matches supply with demand
- **Disintermediation risk**: Once buyers and sellers connect, what prevents them from transacting off-platform?

## Take Rate Benchmarks

- **Physical goods**: 10-20% (Etsy 6.5%, eBay 12-15%, Amazon 15%)
- **Digital goods/services**: 15-30% (App Store 30%, Fiverr 20%, Udemy 37-75%)
- **Ride-hailing/delivery**: 20-30% (Uber 25%, DoorDash 15-30%)
- **Real estate/high-value**: 1-6% (Airbnb 3-15%, Zillow referral 1%)
- **Financial services**: 0.5-3% (payment processors, lending marketplaces)

## Growth Patterns

- **Constrained launch**: Start in one geography or vertical, achieve density, then expand
- **Supply seeding**: Manually onboard initial supply (Uber drove to car dealerships, Airbnb photographed apartments)
- **Managed marketplace**: Start with high-touch, manual matching, then automate
- **SaaS-first**: Provide tools to one side first (supply), then add marketplace layer
- **Network effects**: Each new participant makes the platform more valuable for all others

## Key Risks

- **Multi-homing**: Users active on multiple competing platforms simultaneously
- **Winner-take-all dynamics**: Marketplace categories tend toward 1-2 dominant players
- **Regulatory**: Many marketplace categories face emerging regulation (gig economy, short-term rentals)
- **Trust & safety**: Marketplaces must handle disputes, fraud, and quality control
- **Subsidy dependency**: Growth driven by unsustainable discounts or incentives
